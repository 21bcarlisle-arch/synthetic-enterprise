## 1. Texas ERCOT February 2021 — Anatomy and UK Analogue

**What happened:** Winter Storm Uri knocked out ~30 GW of Texas generation (gas wells/pipes froze; wind turbines iced). ERCOT islanded — no significant AC interconnection to neighbouring US grids. System-wide offer cap held at $9,000/MWh for **77 consecutive hours** (midnight Feb 15 to morning Feb 19). Normal Texas spot: $25–50/MWh. Total event damage: at least $26.5bn. Retailer Luminant lost ~$1bn. Griddy customers on variable tariffs faced individual bills of $5,000+ for five days. Multiple retailers declared bankruptcy; $16bn in charges later found excessive.

**Root causes:**
- No mandatory winterisation of gas infrastructure
- Islanded grid with no emergency import capacity
- Pure energy-only market (price cap is sole investment incentive)

**UK equivalent scenario:** Cannot directly replicate — UK has price cap, nine interconnectors, Capacity Market. But the analogue exists: **sustained January cold snap + low wind + Norwegian export interruption.** January 2026 cold snap was a proof of concept:
- UK demand peaked 47.3 GW (highest since March 2018)
- Wind fell to 2 GW on January 8
- NESO bought back exports from Nemo, BritNed, Viking simultaneously
- Wholesale prices hit **£1,040/MWh** around 13:00 January 5
- Dutch imbalance prices topped €4,000/MWh

That lasted hours because: (a) price cap shields retail consumers, (b) interconnectors partially stabilise the system. A "UK ERCOT moment" would require:
- Gas storage depletion (UK holds ~3.1 bcm = 10–16 days winter demand)
- Simultaneous Norwegian import failure (~25% of UK supply)
- Three or more days wind below 5 GW

Price spike would be retail-insulated but would cascade into supplier insolvencies via unhedged wholesale exposure.

## 2. Three Distinct Failure Modes (California/Germany/UK compared)

**California 2020 — Capacity adequacy failure:**
CAISO called emergency rotating blackouts August 14–15 2020 (first involuntary since 2001). Root cause: record heatwave demand while thermal baseload (gas, nuclear) retired faster than storage/demand response could replace it. Insufficient dispatchable capacity during peak heat coinciding with low renewable output. No commodity price crisis — a physical capacity gap.

**German Dunkelflaute — Renewable intermittency event:**
November/December 2024: multi-week period of simultaneous low wind and solar. German day-ahead prices exceeded €145/MWh, peaking at €175/MWh. Germany avoided blackouts through interconnector imports and dispatchable backup. Market signal worked — industrial users on spot exposure faced severe cost pressure.

**UK 2021-22 Gas Crisis — Commodity price transmission event:**
No physical shortage. Gas kept flowing. NBP rose from ~50p/therm (early 2021) to >450p/therm (December 2021). Fed through electricity prices (gas-fired generation sets marginal price). Killed 28 retail energy suppliers — they had sold fixed-price tariffs without hedging; rising wholesale costs exceeded price cap revenue.

**The key distinction:** California = capacity gap; dunkelflaute = renewable intermittency + inadequate storage; UK 2021-22 = commodity price shock with no physical supply failure. UK's Clean Power 2030 trajectory (less gas backup) increases dunkelflaute exposure and California-type capacity adequacy risk simultaneously.

## 3. Gas Supply Geopolitical Disruption Scenarios

**UK gas supply (2025):**
- Domestic UKCS: ~24.5 bcm
- Norwegian pipeline: ~45–50 bcm
- LNG imports: ~13.3 bcm
- Some Dutch/Belgian transit

**Norwegian failure scenario:** Norway offshore directorate projects two of three production scenarios showing steep post-2026 decline. A Troll/Åsgard/Ormen Lange infrastructure failure could remove 10–15 bcm from annual UK supply quickly. UK statutory security of supply tests "loss of the single biggest supply source" — required supply margin fell from 127 mcm/day to 83 mcm/day year-on-year as domestic production declines.

**Strait of Hormuz / LNG disruption (already happened):** 2 March 2026: Strait of Hormuz crisis caused European gas prices to surge from ~€30/MWh to >€60/MWh in 48 hours. Europe gets 12–14% of LNG via Qatari shipments through the strait. UK LNG imports projected to reach 46% of total supply by 2035 as North Sea production falls to ~5.4 bcm by 2036 — this scenario becomes structurally more severe over time. ICIS Foresight modelling: a cold snap in 2028/29 raises NBP day-ahead to €30.4/MWh (77p/therm) under stable UKCS; under major LNG disruption the multiplier would be substantially larger.

## 4. Battery Storage at Scale: Price Dynamics at 20–30 GW

UK Clean Power 2030: 23–27 GW short-duration battery storage by 2030; 55 GW by 2035. Current deployed: ~6–8 GW.

**What 6 GW has already done:** Frequency response prices collapsed 7-fold since 2022 — FCR revenues fell from >90% of BESS revenue in 2021 to ~20% by 2024 as market saturated.

**What 20–30 GW would do to spot prices (UCL Bartlett + Modo Energy):**
- Compress price spike peaks (batteries discharge into high-price periods, suppressing peaks)
- Deepen price troughs (all batteries charge simultaneously in low-price periods)
- April 2026 solar data already shows negative pricing at **−£29.27/MWh** during 16.9% of midday half-hours
- As battery capacity scales, the £22/MWh midday/evening spread compresses
- A 1 GW four-hour battery cycling daily: ~£32M/GW/year gross arbitrage at current spreads — falls as scale increases
- Returns normalise to 8–10% by 2030 (from current higher levels)

**The "race to charge" synchronisation risk:** All batteries charging 11:00–14:00 on sunny low-demand days, then discharging simultaneously into evening peak. System operators need intraday balancing tools beyond current Balancing Mechanism.

## 5. Interconnector Dependency and Net Exporter Scenarios

**Current state (year to Sep 2025):**
- UK imported 43 TWh, exported 12 TWh — net importer but with complex intraday flows
- Volume-weighted import price £41.69/MWh; export £38.54/MWh
- Three cables (BritNed, Nemo, Viking) cycle intraday — importing 2,135 MW at 10:00, reversing to ~−1,469 MW overnight
- France nuclear maintenance 2022: UK briefly became net exporter

**Net exporter scenario (2030s):** Ofgem + NESO: GB will become a structural net electricity exporter by early 2030s as wind/solar outpaces domestic demand. Five more interconnectors approved, due by 2032 (>1.2 GW/year additional). UK becomes low-price zone during surplus periods.

**EU structural high-price scenario:** If EU carbon pricing (ETS) rises well above UK Carbon Price Support, or EU gas supply tightens, continental markets pull UK generation eastward through interconnectors — tightening UK domestic supply and raising spot prices. Strathclyde analysis: additional interconnectors reduce GB prices in normal conditions but can create perverse effects when continental markets are tight.

**January 2026 cold snap proof of concept:** When UK needed to import during the cold snap, all three cycling interconnectors reversed simultaneously (NESO had to buy back scheduled exports). Interconnector buyback hit **£1,040/MWh** — demonstrating that interconnector capacity can be absorbed rapidly and bidirectional reliance creates correlated UK-EU grid stress events.

## 6. Ofgem Stress Testing — What They Actually Publish

**Capital requirements (March 2025):**
- Capital Target: £115 adjusted net assets per dual-fuel-equivalent customer
- Capital Floor: £0 (non-negative adjusted net assets)
- These are STOCK measures, not flow/price-scenario tests

**Electricity reliability standard:** LOLE 3 hours/year. Winter 2025/26 actual forecast: <0.1 hours.

**Gas stress scenarios:** 1-in-20-year peak daily demand and 1-in-50-year severe winter as outer bounds. Winter 2025/26: supply margin 83 mcm/day at 1-in-20-year demand (down from 127 mcm prior year). Framework requires coverage of "loss of the single biggest supply source."

**What Ofgem does NOT publish:** Specific wholesale price spike magnitudes, duration parameters, or named scenarios used in individual supplier stress assessments — these are confidential. The April 2025 Financial Resilience Transparency Report confirms stress tests occur but scenarios are not publicly disclosed.

**Structural gap:** Price cap means retail consumers are insulated from wholesale spikes — but creates regulatory asymmetry where suppliers bear basis risk between hedging positions and cap allowances. Ofgem's capital floor/target approach is a static snapshot measure, not a dynamic stress scenario with quantified price paths.

## Simulation Scenario Generation Implications

**What the real-world research tells us to model:**

1. **Regime-switching price distribution** — not Gaussian; bimodal at high renewable penetration (very low/negative during surplus; very high during droughts). 2027 is the projected tipping point.

2. **Correlated stress events** — UK cold snap + Norwegian supply disruption + EU-wide dunkelflaute can all hit simultaneously. Independence assumption fails badly in tails.

3. **Demand-side transition** — EV smart charging bifurcation (7 GW peak vs 1 GW overnight depending on participation rates); heat pump adoption creating new winter demand shape.

4. **Gas/electricity coupling** — not independent commodities; heat pump adoption destroys gas volume while increasing electricity demand; network death spiral is a real tail risk.

5. **Storage market saturation** — at 20–30 GW BESS, price spread compression changes the economics of arbitrage-funded suppliers and shifts the grid balancing dynamic.

6. **Interconnector-as-amplifier** — at moments of high UK stress (cold snap, dunkelflaute), interconnectors can AMPLIFY the price spike by simultaneously reversing direction, not dampen it.
