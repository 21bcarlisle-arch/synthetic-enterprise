About

Poesys is not building a model of an energy supplier; it is building a running autonomous energy business -- one whose commercial, financial, and operational processes all execute, settled continuously against real Elexon/NESO half-hourly settlement data (2016-2025). The company cannot see inside the simulation it runs in: it discovers its world only through what a real supplier could observe -- meter reads, market feeds, customer contacts, bills and payments, regulatory publications. The test on every line of company code is simple: "could a real UK supplier know this?" The final phase of the roadmap is a go-live analysis -- what it would actually take, legally and operationally, to serve a real customer.

It is also a company that can die. Insolvency and licence revocation are terminal states here, not exceptions designed away -- which is what makes the crisis-survival evidence below an earned result, not an assumption: roughly 30 real UK suppliers failed in the 2021-22 crisis, and this one did not.

Three products come out of this, in order: the Method first (the transferable way of directing an autonomous agent to build a company -- the casebook is what makes the rest repeatable), the synthetic data second, and the Platform (the software itself) third. A live, public track record of predictions vs outcomes -- misses included -- runs on the Method page.

Key Discoveries

Regime-Change Blindness

Agent converged to near-naked hedging during 2016-2020 calm, directly before the crisis -- mirroring how 28 real UK suppliers failed in 2021-22. Discovered in simulation before the fix.

Activity-Based Pricing Gap

Flat margin pricing makes high-cost-to-serve customers net-negative. Large SME customers destroy value under standard pricing.

Price Elasticity Disconfirmed

Rising prices do NOT increase switching propensity. 2022 crisis bills (avg £3,549) produced the lowest switching rate (3-4%). Savings available -- not price level -- drives switching.

Crisis Survival

The supplier survived 2021-22 with treasury drawdown, churn spike, and recovery. 30+ real suppliers failed. The simulation replicates the conditions faithfully.

The Epochs

Not a static checklist -- the real, dependency-ordered story of what this business becomes next, and why each stage has to happen before the one after it.

Epoch 1 -- Built (depth before scale)

A single fixed cast of customers, run once end-to-end against real 2016-2025 settlement data, with a company layer detailed enough to price, hedge, bill, collect, and survive a real crisis on its own logic. This is the foundation the next two epochs build on, not a finished product.

Epoch 2 -- Closing the Wall (next up)

The company is supposed to know its world only the way a real supplier would -- through meter reads, bills, and customer contact -- never by peeking at simulation ground truth. A desk audit this month found the wall holds in most places, but not all: the customer portal reads physical-property "truth" (home type, EPC rating, smart-meter status) straight out of the same list the simulation itself uses, with no discovery step in between. That gap already produced a real, live bug -- one customer's smart-meter flag defaulting wrong because the field it read had gone missing. Separately, every run today plays the identical 27-customer cast with the identical seeded outcomes: there is no lever to vary who the company serves, which means the tournament in Epoch 3 has nothing to run yet. Closing both gaps is the precondition for everything after it.

●Customer truth discovered through interfaces only -- no company code reads simulation-adjacent data directly
●Population becomes a per-run draw from a real distribution, not a fixed cast
○Generation and validation independently anchored by design, not by accident
○A real two-clock financial ledger (billed / settled / banked) replacing today's single-computed-value model
○Governance and an ex-post margin bridge layered onto the pricing engine that already exists

Epoch 3 -- The Company That Can Die (director-decided direction, re-rank input, not yet queued)

Five mission clauses, decided directly with the director, define what this business becomes once the wall is closed. Insolvency and licence revocation stop being avoided outcomes and become real endings: the endgame is a longevity tournament -- repeated lifetime runs, scored by survival duration times cumulative value, a league table of strategies competing on robustness. A run that dies doesn't end the project -- the corpse becomes data for the next one. Brand stops being decoration and becomes behavioural physics: it changes the discount required to move a customer, absorbs one service failure for free, and remembers a second one with a permanently lower switching threshold -- discovered by the company from its own win/loss data, never read as a parameter. Learning happens under regulated experimentation: the same consumer-duty and fairness constraints a real regulator would enforce, so the tension between learning fast and staying compliant is a designed feature, not an oversight. Every random number in the world has to earn its distribution from a published statistic first (anchored noise), and nothing happens instantly -- every process carries a real latency distribution, so failed direct debits, callbacks that never come, and appointment no-shows are counted as first-class cost, not edge cases (temporal realism).

○Longevity tournament: survival x value scoring, a league table of strategies
○Brand as behavioural physics: elasticity, forgiveness buffer, sensitised exit, discovered not read
○Regulated experimentation: learning fenced by the same rules a real regulator would hold it to
○Anchored noise: every distribution sourced from real published data or honestly flagged
○Temporal realism: latency distributions everywhere, doom loops as first-class cost
○The Belief Book: a company hypothesis register, "time-to-truth" as the headline learning metric
○Board model: owner sets mission + reserved matters, company proposes strategy, the scenario battery is the exam

Key Regulatory Findings

WHD / ECO Exemption

Both mandatory only for >150k domestic customers. Portfolio has 2–5 residential customers -- EXEMPT for all years. Growth warning: obligation triggers at ~150k customers.

CCL 2019 Spike

April 2019: electricity CCL +45%, gas CCL +67%. Largest single-year policy cost increase in the dataset. I&C pass-through means this hits customers directly.

FiT Scheme Closure

FiT closed to new applicants April 2019. Board section shows cost falling to NIL from 2020. Smart Export Guarantee replaced it but is not a supplier levy.

FRA Capital Post-2022

Ofgem FRA requires combined market + credit stress. Model: equity > (price VaR + credit stress). Board RAG shows FRA compliance per year 2016–2025.
