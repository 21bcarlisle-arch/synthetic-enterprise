Why weather matters: Heating Degree Days (HDD) measure cumulative heating demand. The UK National Grid uses a base of 15.5°C — each degree below that per day adds one HDD unit. Cold winters drive gas demand sharply higher, tighten storage, and push up wholesale prices. The 2021–22 crisis combined depleted storage (Rough closed 2017) with a cold winter, producing record prices that forced around 30 UK suppliers to exit the market.

Monthly System Short % — 2016–2025
Short% and NIV, for outsiders: Elexon's Net Imbalance Volume (NIV) is the gap between what generators said they'd produce and what the system actually needed each settlement period; when NIV is negative the system is short — National Grid ESO had to call on expensive backup generation to keep the lights on. Short % is the share of settlement periods each month where the system was short. A high Short % month is a tight, expensive month — the same tightness that shows up as a price spike on the Prices tab.

Customer Behavioural Model
Four-dimension customer model — each simulated customer has a physical profile (EPC band, heating system, appliances), an economic trajectory (income stress driven by life events), a behavioural model (payment timing, switching propensity), and an emotional accumulator (satisfaction score decaying monthly, shocked by bill spikes). The company layer cannot see these simulation internals directly — it observes only meter reads, payment records, and its own billing data.

Switching Propensity Distribution Over Time — the Vulnerability Trap
Financially stressed customers switch LESS, not more, even when unhappy (`simulation/switching_propensity.py`): friction costs (deposits, direct debit setup, mental load) suppress switching harder for HIGH-stress households, while LOW-stress customers have the bandwidth to shop around. Derived here from each customer's own income-stress trajectory — no separate SIM field, the multiplier (LOW ×1.10, MODERATE ×0.85, HIGH ×0.65) is a fixed function of stress.
Customer Journey Stages — the Behavioural Pulse
Journey state machine (Phase QL): every customer sits in one of four stages at any point — content (no reason to look elsewhere), irritated (a friction event has landed, e.g. a bill shock), in-market (actively considering switching), comparing (evaluating a specific alternative). A renewal resets the cycle. This is the sim's real-time behavioural pulse — not a survey, an emergent state derived from life events, price shocks and satisfaction.

Meter-Read Delay & Estimation — Unhappy-Path Physics
Phase 3 (`simulation/meter_reads.py`): settlement data isn't instant or perfect — a real supplier only bills off an ACTUAL meter read if one arrives in time. Smart meters transmit automatically (small delay, occasional WAN "not communicating" dropout); traditional meters rely on a self-submitted or periodic read, so most periods without one are ESTIMATED from the customer's own trailing history. The tail of this delay distribution is exactly where estimated bills come from.

Event Frequency — Life Events, Bill Shocks, Payment Misses, Switches
How often does anything actually happen to a customer? Four independently-tracked event streams, each per year: life events (job loss, house move — `simulation/life_events.py`), bill shocks (a >20% unit-rate rise at renewal — `run_phase2b.py`'s `_NG_BILL_SHOCK_THRESHOLD`), payment misses (LATE + DD_FAILED, from the full per-event payment record history — `company/crm/payment_behaviour_analytics.py`), switches (realized churn events), and complaints (Phase RU: `simulation/feedback_survey.py` dispatches occurrence + resolution timing off the same bill-shock signal, resolved against the real 56-day Ombudsman SLC window).

Satisfaction vs Complaints
Phase RU: mean SIM-true satisfaction (from every dispatched survey event, not just responders) against complaints raised that year (`reputation_events`, `simulation/feedback_survey.py` dispatch_complaint_and_resolution). Complaints are rare in this portfolio (13 across the full decade) so the yearly count is a thin signal, but it is a real one, not a fabricated zero.
Both Sides of the Wall — SIM Truth vs Company-Observed
The epistemic wall, at portfolio scale: every renewal records both the SIM's ground-truth churn probability and the company's own model estimate (`churn_accuracy_by_renewal`, the same field behind the C_IC1 flagship divergence case on the Customers tab). Aggregated across all customers and years, this line pair is literally what the company cannot see vs what it estimates instead — the gap between the two lines IS the wall.
True Satisfaction vs Measured CSAT (Phase RU)
The same wall, for satisfaction: the company never reads `sim_satisfaction_score` directly — only whatever a customer chooses to report on a solicited CSAT survey. Response propensity is U-shaped (very satisfied and very dissatisfied customers respond; the silent middle mostly doesn't — `simulation/feedback_survey.py::response_propensity`), so the measured line is a biased sample of the true one, not a clean read of it.
Nudge Physics — Retention Rate by Offer Framing and Susceptibility (Layer 1)
NUDGE_PHYSICS.md Layer 1: every retention offer carries a framing_type (loss_framed / gain_framed) the company itself chose as a comms cohort split — observable. Each customer also has a hidden loss-aversion susceptibility (loss_averse / gain_responsive / neutral, `simulation/nudge_physics.py`) the company never reads. This panel is the SIM's own verification that the physics is real: retention rate should be higher whenever framing_type matches the customer's hidden disposition, and roughly flat for neutral customers. The company's own discovered version of this (framing lift by segment, without the susceptibility column) is on the Supplier tab's Nudge Discovery section.
